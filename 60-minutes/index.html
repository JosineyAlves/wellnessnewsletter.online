For decades, cognitive decline has been treated as an inevitable part of aging. But a growing body of neurological research suggests that assumption may be incomplete.

Some experts now believe the issue doesn't start with age itself… but with something far more subtle: what could be described as "brain starvation."

The human brain depends on specific nutrients — particularly certain lipids — to maintain memory, clarity, and neural connections. Yet over the past several decades, widely adopted dietary changes may have significantly reduced these essential elements.

The result?

A gradual decline that, until recently, was considered irreversible.

What's most intriguing is this:

When researchers examined individuals who maintained sharp cognitive function well into their 80s and 90s, they found a consistent pattern — these individuals did not follow the same dietary restrictions commonly recommended today.

Which raises an uncomfortable, but necessary question:

What if part of what we've been told protects the brain… is actually contributing to its decline?

In the video above, a neurologist explains how this insight led to a protocol built around three specific brain lipids — and why this approach is gaining attention for its impact in cases once believed to have no solution.
